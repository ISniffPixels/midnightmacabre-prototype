What Is IT?

Stephen King's It, particularly the character of Pennywise the Dancing Clown, has become one of the most iconic and terrifying figures in horror literature. The character is both a manifestation of primal fear and an embodiment of something far more ancient and malevolent. Here’s an in-depth exploration of the origins and nature of It.

At the core of the story is It, also known as Pennywise the Dancing Clown, but in reality, It is a far more complex and ancient entity. King reveals that It is not simply a supernatural villain but an extraterrestrial being from another dimension. It originated from a void known as the Macroverse (also called the Todash Darkness in King’s universe), which predates our universe and exists outside of time and space.

In essence, It is an ancient cosmic entity that has existed for billions of years. Its real form, the one that humans cannot perceive or comprehend, is a shifting mass of deadlights. When a human looks into these deadlights, they become insane or catatonic, overwhelmed by its incomprehensible nature. Pennywise is simply one of the many forms that It takes, chosen because clowns are a figure that evokes fear in many children. However, It can manifest as anything that its victim fears most, whether it’s a werewolf, a giant bird, or something personal from their life.

The Creation of It

It, published in 1986, is often considered one of Stephen King's most ambitious and intricate novels. It’s a sprawling epic that blends horror, coming-of-age elements, and a deep exploration of childhood trauma. The story is set in the fictional town of Derry, Maine, a place with a history steeped in violence, disaster, and mysterious disappearances. King has stated in interviews that It was born out of his desire to write a story that tapped into the universal fears of childhood—especially the feeling of dread that is often associated with clowns, a motif he uses to terrifying effect.

Pennywise is the main villain in the novel, a monstrous being that can shape-shift into whatever its victim fears the most. For many, it takes the form of a clown, a being that is at once familiar and unsettling. This duality is a key theme in the novel—It is something that seems harmless on the surface but is terrifying beneath the facade.

The Clown Persona

Pennywise the Dancing Clown is perhaps the most iconic and enduring manifestation of It. King chose a clown because of its duality—clowns are meant to be funny and entertaining, but there’s something deeply unsettling about them. They wear masks of exaggerated happiness, yet they can easily turn sinister. For children, this juxtaposition is particularly disturbing, making Pennywise the perfect predator.

Throughout the novel, Pennywise taunts and terrorizes the children of Derry, most notably the Losers’ Club—a group of seven kids who become the primary protagonists. Pennywise lures them in with a false sense of safety, often using balloons, jokes, and playful antics before revealing his true malevolent nature. This sense of manipulation is key to Pennywise’s character. He thrives on fear, feeding on the terror of his victims, which makes the children’s fear an essential part of his existence.

Though Pennywise’s most iconic form is a clown, It can take many forms depending on the individual’s deepest fears. For example, in the novel, Pennywise transforms into a giant bird for Mike Hanlon, a leper for Eddie Kaspbrak, and a mummy for Ben Hanscom. It uses these manifestations to weaken its victims emotionally and mentally before killing them. The clown, however, is the recurring symbol of this entity's evil throughout the novel.

The Feeding Ground

Derry is not just a setting but also a character in its own right. Stephen King portrays the town as an inherently evil place, with a dark history of violence, tragedy, and disaster. There is a cyclical nature to the horrors in Derry—approximately every 27 years, It awakens from a long period of hibernation, and when it does, a wave of terror and death follows.

King describes It as a predator that feeds on children’s fear, and Derry provides a perfect environment for that. The town’s adults are portrayed as passive, indifferent, or even complicit in the horrors that unfold, either by choice or by It’s influence. Many horrific events, such as the explosion at the Kitchener Ironworks and the Bradley Gang shootout, are tied to It’s awakenings, suggesting that the entity not only feeds on fear but also actively manipulates events to create chaos and destruction.

The Losers’ Club becomes aware of this cycle when they investigate Derry’s past and realize that similar patterns of death and disaster have occurred throughout its history. They piece together that It has been living in Derry for centuries, predating the town’s founding, and is responsible for much of its tragedy.

Stephen King’s It is a complex exploration of fear, trauma, and the battle between good and evil. Pennywise the Dancing Clown, the most iconic manifestation of It, represents a timeless and terrifying force that feeds on human fear, particularly that of children. But beyond the physical horrors of Pennywise, King’s novel delves into the psychological and emotional scars left by childhood trauma, suggesting that while monsters may be defeated, the scars they leave behind remain.

At its core, It is a story about the power of friendship, the resilience of the human spirit, and the courage it takes to confront our deepest fears—whether they come in the form of a clown or the traumas of our past. Through the lens of horror, King paints a poignant picture of what it means to grow up and the demons, both literal and metaphorical, that we must face along the way.
